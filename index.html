CAL Ninth Annual Actuarial Case Competition

Best Solution (Health & Benefits, Property & Casualty)

UCLA students Lawrence Chen, Brandon Cheng, Serene Supakkul, and I represented UCLA at UC Berkeley's California Actuarial League's Case Competition Spring 2018. Over the course of several months, we wrote a business proposal covering three distinct cases: H&B, P&C, and Retirement. In H&B, we were tasked with pricing adverse selection and analyzing the effects of offering a standalone plan as multi-choice options. In the P&C case, we analyzed the adequacy of different rating variables and modified them, all while attempting to minimize policy-level premium dislocation. Finally, in the retirement case, we analyzed the retirement preparedness of an individual, performing sensitivity analyses on our many assumptions.

We were selected as finalists for the H&B and P&C tracks, so we took a day trip up to Berkeley to present our recommendations in two 15-minute presentations. After our proposal, we successfully defended our conclusions in two 15-minute question and answer segments, during which the judges asked questions about how we conducted our analyses and how we arrived at our results. We're proud to have represented UCLA well at this competition!

BAS Excel and VBA Workshops

During my time as Director of Professional Development of Bruin Actuarial Society, I revamped the series of technical workshops we offer to our members. In an effort to engage members with truly actuarial examples, I took it upon myself to write a workshop similar to an actuarial case, touching on the crucial concepts of pricing and reserving.

To write the actuarial examples, I wrote VBA code to simulate tens of thousands of policy and claim data from the probability distributions I learned about in CAS Exam 1. With Poisson frequencies and gamma severities, I was able to generate realistic data to use as examples, thus exposing members to Excel, pricing, and reserving, all at once.

BAS Access and SQL Workshops

As a continuation of my efforts to expose members to developments within the actuarial field, I brought SQL workshops back to BAS. Currently, these workshops teach SQL through Microsoft Access, since this is most accessible to students. However, the skills are easily transferable to any SQL server language or any extension of SQL.

When writing these workshops, I used the same policy and claim data I simulated for the Excel workshops I wrote. In fact, I found that while writing the Excel workshops, many of the calculations I wanted to perform were much more easily done in SQL than in Excel. When we go from the tens of thousands of records in my workshops to the millions of records in industry, database management is even more crucial, which is why I wanted to offer this workshop.
